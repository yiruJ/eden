## Marketing Psychology — Applied to Eden

**Endowment Effect:** The trial lesson is the product sample. Once families experience a lesson, they feel invested and are reluctant to leave. Emphasise the trial as low-risk entry, not a sales step.

**Loss Aversion:** "Don't miss the window when children learn fastest" — ages 4–7 is the optimal musical development period. Frame delay as a cost.

**Social Proof:** Parent testimonials > generic reviews. Specific ("my daughter cried when lessons were cancelled") > vague ("great teacher!"). Collect and display these.

**Authority:** Teachers' credentials and years of experience build trust. Mention classical training background without being stuffy.

**Reciprocity:** A free trial creates obligation. Name it clearly: "Book a free trial — no payment, no obligation."

**Scarcity/Commitment:** Limited spots per teacher. "Enrolments are limited" is real — use it without fabricating urgency.

**Mere Exposure / Rule of 7:** Parents often need 3–7 touchpoints before booking. Retargeting ads, follow-up emails after enquiry, and consistent local presence all compound.

**Regret Aversion:** "Don't look back and wish you'd started earlier" works especially well for Korean-speaking families where classical training carries cultural weight.

## Brand Voice & Tone

**Personality:** Warm, encouraging, quietly confident — like a trusted local expert, not a corporate brand
**Formality:** Professional but approachable — write how a caring teacher would speak to a parent
**Avoid:** Cold corporate language, over-promising, pushy sales tone, exclamation marks, em dashes (—), hyphens used as separators (e.g. "Lessons - Book Now"). These are AI writing tells and make copy feel generic.
**Eden's tone in one sentence:** "We care about your child's relationship with music, not just their technique."

**For Korean-language copy:** Respectful, slightly more formal than English equivalent. Emphasise teacher credentials and structured progression — values that resonate with Korean educational culture.
